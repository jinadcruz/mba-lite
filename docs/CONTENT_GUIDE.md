# Content Guide — MBA Lite

This guide defines the standards for creating lessons, case studies, and knowledge checks for MBA Lite.

## Content Philosophy

Every piece of content should pass three tests: Is it accurate? Is it accessible? Is it actionable? We are distilling complex MBA concepts for busy professionals. Clarity always wins over academic precision. If a concept requires jargon, define it immediately. If a framework is abstract, ground it in a real example.

## Lesson Structure

Each daily lesson consists of four components delivered in order.

### 1. Concept Explainer (4–5 minutes reading time)

Target word count: 600–800 words. Start with a one-sentence hook that connects the concept to a real business situation. Introduce the framework or concept with a clear definition. Explain why it matters using a concrete example. Walk through the mechanics (with a diagram or formula if quantitative). End with a "So What?" paragraph connecting the concept back to the user's career.

Formatting: Use subheadings sparingly (max 2). Use diagrams for any spatial or sequential concept. Use tables for comparisons. Never use more than one formula per explainer, and always provide a plain-English interpretation alongside it.

### 2. Case Study (4–5 minutes reading time)

Target word count: 500–800 words. Every case study follows this structure:

- **The Company** (1 paragraph) — Who they are, where they operate, what they do
- **The Situation** (1–2 paragraphs) — What challenge or decision they faced
- **The Decision** (1–2 paragraphs) — What they chose to do and why
- **The Outcome** (1 paragraph) — What happened as a result
- **The Framework Connection** (1 paragraph) — How today's concept explains or predicts the outcome
- **Discussion Prompt** (1 question) — "What would you have done differently?"

Case studies must be based on publicly available information. Cite your sources at the bottom. Never reproduce copyrighted material. All analysis must be original.

### 3. Knowledge Check (2–3 minutes)

Five questions per lesson. Question types: multiple choice (3–4 options), scenario-based ("You are the CEO of..."), and framework application ("Which of Porter's Five Forces is most relevant here?").

Every question must include an explanation for the correct answer and a brief note on why each wrong answer is wrong. Questions should test application, not memorization.

### 4. AI Tutor Prompt

Each lesson includes a suggested opening prompt for the AI tutor. This is not shown to the user directly — it is used by the AI tutor system to initiate Socratic dialogue when the user enters the tutor after completing the lesson.

Example: "The user just learned about DCF valuation and read a case study about Spotify's IPO. Ask them what discount rate they would use for a high-growth tech company and why."

## Case Study Diversity Requirements

At least 40% of case studies must feature non-US companies. Target distribution:

- North America: 30%
- Europe: 20%
- Asia-Pacific: 25%
- Africa: 10%
- Latin America: 10%
- Middle East: 5%

Every module must include at least two case studies from emerging markets.

## Voice and Tone

Write as a knowledgeable colleague, not a textbook. Be conversational but precise. Use "you" to address the learner directly. Avoid hedging language ("it could be argued that..."). State things clearly and let the complexity come from the ideas, not the sentence structure.

Avoid: "The concept of synergy, which was first articulated by H. Igor Ansoff in 1965, refers to the phenomenon whereby..."
Prefer: "Synergy means 1 + 1 = 3. When two companies merge, the combined value should exceed what they're worth separately. Here's how to tell if that's actually happening."

## Quality Checklist

Before submitting any content for review:

- [ ] Concept explainer passes the "explain to a smart friend" test
- [ ] Case study is based on verifiable, publicly available facts
- [ ] Case study sources are cited
- [ ] No copyrighted material is reproduced
- [ ] Knowledge check questions test application, not recall
- [ ] All five knowledge check questions have correct explanations
- [ ] Geographic diversity requirements are met across the module
- [ ] Diagrams are clear, labeled, and accessible (alt text included)
- [ ] Word counts are within target ranges
- [ ] Content has been reviewed by at least one subject matter expert
